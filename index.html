Marie Curie

The woman who revolutionized science

Here's a time line of Marie Curie's life:

  • 1867 - Born as Maria Skłodowska in Warsaw, Poland, the youngest of five children
  • 1883 - Graduates from secondary school with a gold medal, showing early academic excellence
  • 1885 - Works as a governess to help support her sister's medical studies in Paris, while secretly attending the "Flying University," an underground Polish institute of higher learning
  • 1891 - Moves to Paris to study physics and mathematics at the Sorbonne, living in a cold attic and often subsisting on bread and tea
  • 1893 - Earns her physics degree, graduating first in her class
  • 1894 - Earns a mathematics degree. Meets Pierre Curie, who would become her husband and research partner
  • 1895 - Marries Pierre Curie. Together they begin their pioneering work on radiation
  • 1897 - Gives birth to her first daughter Irène while continuing her research
  • 1898 - Discovers two new elements: polonium (named after her native Poland) and radium
  • 1903 - Becomes the first woman to win a Nobel Prize, sharing the Physics Prize with Pierre and Henri Becquerel for their work on radiation
  • 1904 - Welcomes second daughter Eve and sets up a laboratory with Pierre
  • 1906 - Pierre dies tragically in a street accident. Marie takes over his position as professor at the Sorbonne, becoming the first woman to teach there
  • 1911 - Wins a second Nobel Prize, this time in Chemistry, for her continued research on radioactivity. Becomes the first person to win Nobel Prizes in multiple sciences
  • 1914 - Establishes mobile X-ray units during World War I, personally driving them to the front lines
  • 1921 - Tours the United States, where she receives a gram of radium for her research, funded by donations from American women
  • 1922 - Becomes a member of the French Academy of Medicine for her work on radiotherapy
  • 1932 - Establishes the Radium Institute in Warsaw, which becomes a major center for scientific research
  • 1934 - Dies at age 66 from aplastic anemia, caused by long-term exposure to radiation

"Marie Curie is still the only woman to win two Nobel Prizes. Her life and achievements serve as a testament to the power of intellect and determination in advancing human knowledge and improving lives through science."

-- A legacy that continues through the Marie Skłodowska-Curie Actions fellowship program, supporting thousands of researchers worldwide
